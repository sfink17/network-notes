Vint Cerf

The Father of the Internet

Born in New Haven, Coneticut as Vinton Gray Cerf on June 23rd, 1943, Vint is a very well-dressed pioneer of the internet. Coined by many as one of the "fathers of the internet", Vint, along with Robert Kahn, created the TCP/IP Protocol which is still widely used today.

Vint started out with a Bachelor Degree of Science in Mathematics from Stanford University when he went to work as a systems engineer at IBM. After working for 2 years, Vint went to the University of California Los Angelos to get his Masters, then Doctorate, degree in Computer Science. While at UCLA, Vint worked with a group of people who together created the first two nodes of the Internet's predecessor, the ARPAnet. After gaining his doctorate, Vint went back to Stanford University, where he became an assistant professor to research network connections. While he was there, he met Robert Kahn. Together, Vint and Robert designed the TCP/IP protocol for the United States Department of Defense. At this point, Vint moved to the Defense Advanced Research Projects Agency, another agency of the United States Government. After a couple years, he moved to MCI Communications to serve as Vice President, and oversee the development of the first e-mail system, MCI Mail.

In 1998, Vint assisted in the foundation of the Internet Corporation for Assigned Names and Numbers, which is a nonprofit organization that manages the root DNS servers among other services related to internet namespaces. One year later, Vint joined ICANN as a member of the board. In November 2000, he was elected as chairman of the ICANN board, where he remained for 7 years before resigning in November 2007.
